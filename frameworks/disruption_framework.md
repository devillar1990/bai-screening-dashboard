# B.AI Disruption Risk Assessment Framework (v4)

## Framework Overview

The v4 Disruption Risk framework is aligned with the Brightstar.AI Screening Criteria (D2). The framework measures **Revenue (pricing and/or volume) or cost disruption** across 4 dimensions:

1. **Business Model Disruption** — Can AI fundamentally alter or bypass the company's business model?
2. **Value Chain Disruption** — Can AI restructure the industry value chain, disintermediating or commoditizing the company's role?
3. **New AI-Native Products/Services** — Are AI-native alternatives emerging that could replace the company's core offerings?
4. **New AI-Native Entrants** — Are AI-native competitors entering the company's existing product/service market?

## Philosophy

This framework goes far beyond surface-level indicators like public market reactions to AI announcements. Instead, it conducts rigorous, process-level analysis of a company's vulnerability to AI-driven disruption.

The core question is: **Does AI strengthen this company's competitive position, or does it erode it?**

Companies fall on a spectrum:
- **AI Beneficiaries**: Companies where AI strengthens the moat, reduces costs, and widens competitive advantages (e.g., companies with proprietary data, complex operations, or deep customer relationships)
- **AI Victims**: Companies whose core value proposition can be replicated or bypassed by AI (e.g., information intermediaries, low-complexity service providers, or companies whose main asset is labor arbitrage)

Most companies are somewhere in between. The framework helps pinpoint where on this spectrum a target sits.

## Forward-Looking Acceleration Principle

When scoring disruption risk, account for the **exponential pace of AI capability improvement.** Score based on where disruption pressure will realistically be at the **END of a 3-5 year PE hold period**, not solely where it is today. AI capabilities are compounding — what is nascent today may be mainstream in 3 years.

**Practical application:**
- If current disruption pressure is minimal but credible AI research, startup activity, or technology trends suggest material disruption within the hold period, score at least **one level higher** than current-state evidence alone would suggest.
- If a dimension has no visible disruption today AND no plausible AI pathway exists even on a 5-year horizon (e.g., physical services requiring hands-on execution, heavily regulated domains with multi-year approval cycles), the current-state score stands.
- This principle is especially relevant for **Value Chain Disruption** and **New AI-Native Entrants**, where competitive dynamics can shift faster than business model or product disruption.

## Scoring Scale (Disruption Risk)

Each of the 4 Disruption dimensions is scored 1-5 (1 = low pressure, 5 = high pressure):

- **5 - Severe**: Existential disruption pressure in this dimension within 3-5 years
- **4 - High**: Significant disruption pressure. Adaptation required and should be underwritten in investment thesis
- **3 - Moderate**: Material disruption pressure exists. Proactive adaptation needed but manageable
- **2 - Low**: Limited disruption pressure. Mostly incremental change; company can adapt
- **1 - Minimal**: Negligible disruption pressure. Company is well-insulated in this dimension

### Half-Point Scores

Scorers may use half-point increments (1.5, 2.5, 3.5, 4.5) when a company's profile falls between two levels. Use half-points when the company meets some but not all criteria for the next score level. For example:
- Score 2.5 if disruption pressure is between Low and Moderate
- Score 3.5 if disruption pressure is between Moderate and High
- Half-points should be accompanied by explicit evidence of why the company doesn't fully meet the higher threshold

**Total Disruption Score** = Sum of all 4 dimension scores. Range: 4-20.

**Decision Rule:**
- **Low (4-9)**: Limited disruption pressure; mostly incremental change
- **Medium (10-14)**: Material disruption pressure; adaptation required and should be underwritten
- **High (15-20)**: Severe disruption pressure; reinvention or repricing required

## Dimension 1: Business Model Disruption

Assesses whether AI could fundamentally alter or bypass the company's business model, including disintermediation, revenue model fragility, and structural business model threats.

### Decision Rules

**Score 5 — ALL of the following are true:**
- Company is a pure intermediary or broker whose primary function is matching, connecting, or routing (e.g., staffing agencies, insurance brokers, travel agents)
- AI can directly replicate the matching/routing function with equal or better quality
- Revenue model collapses if AI bypasses the intermediary role (e.g., per-transaction or commission-based)
- Active AI-native alternatives already exist and are gaining traction in the market
- No meaningful proprietary data, relationships, or regulatory moats protecting the intermediary position

**Score 4 — AT LEAST THREE of the following are true:**
- Company's intermediary role is weakening as AI enables direct connections
- Revenue model is partially dependent on human labor hours that AI can reduce (e.g., professional services billing by the hour)
- AI-native alternatives exist in adjacent markets and are expanding toward this segment
- Business model adaptations will be required within the 3-5 year hold period
- >30% of revenue comes from activities that AI could automate or disintermediate

**Score 3 — AT LEAST TWO of the following are true:**
- Company adds value beyond pure intermediation but some elements are vulnerable
- AI creates alternative delivery models that compete with the company's approach
- Revenue model faces some compression but is not structurally broken
- Business model evolution needed but core value proposition remains relevant
- 10-30% of revenue from activities with AI substitution risk

**Score 2 — AT LEAST ONE of the following is true:**
- Company is a direct provider (not intermediary) with revenue tied to physical delivery or complex services
- AI cannot bypass the company's role — it only optimizes the company's operations
- Revenue model is tied to physical output, not labor hours or intermediation fees
- <10% of revenue from activities with AI substitution risk

**Score 1 — ALL of the following are true:**
- Company owns the end product/service with no intermediation to bypass
- Revenue model benefits from AI (AI makes operations cheaper, not the company obsolete)
- Physical delivery, regulatory requirements, or deep customer integration make disintermediation implausible
- No AI-native business model can replicate the company's core value creation

**🔮 Forward-looking acceleration prompts for this dimension:**
- Could an AI agent eventually perform the same matching, routing, or advisory function the company provides — even if today's AI can't?
- Are there AI-native startups in adjacent sectors that could pivot into this company's market?
- If the company's value proposition is "expertise + relationships," could AI commoditize the expertise portion within 3-5 years, leaving only relationships as a moat?

## Dimension 2: Value Chain Disruption

Assesses whether AI can restructure the industry value chain in ways that commoditize, disintermediate, or diminish the company's role. This replaces the v3 dimensions of "Pricing/Volume Disruption" and "Cost Shift" with a broader value chain lens.

### Decision Rules

**Score 5 — ALL of the following are true:**
- AI enables end-to-end automation of the company's portion of the value chain, allowing upstream or downstream players to bypass the company entirely
- At least one major value chain participant is actively deploying AI to vertically integrate into the company's domain
- Company's cost structure is dominated by cognitive labor (>40% of costs) that AI can automate, creating a structural cost disadvantage vs. AI-native entrants
- Industry pricing/margins are already compressing as AI-efficient players emerge
- Platform dependency risk: company may become dependent on AI platforms that capture value

**Score 4 — AT LEAST THREE of the following are true:**
- AI enables partial bypass of the company's value chain role (20-40% of value-add at risk)
- Upstream or downstream vertical integration enabled by AI is emerging
- AI-native entrants can achieve 20-40% structural cost advantage over incumbents
- Margin compression of >5pp expected within 3-5 years from AI-driven value chain restructuring
- Company's differentiation on quality/reliability is narrowing as AI improves

**Score 3 — AT LEAST TWO of the following are true:**
- Some value chain restructuring possible but the company retains a defensible role
- AI creates modest pricing efficiency in the market but doesn't fundamentally alter value chain dynamics
- Company can adopt AI itself to maintain competitive positioning (arms race dynamics)
- 2-5pp margin compression possible over hold period from value chain shifts

**Score 2 — AT LEAST ONE of the following is true:**
- Value chain position is protected by physical assets, scale, regulatory licenses, or relationship intensity
- AI optimizes the company's value chain role rather than disrupting it
- Physical cost floor (labor, materials, equipment, fuel) constrains how much AI can restructure the chain
- <2pp margin impact expected from AI-driven value chain changes

**Score 1 — ALL of the following are true:**
- Company's value chain role is physically or regulatorily entrenched and cannot be bypassed by AI
- AI reduces the company's own costs without enabling value chain disintermediation
- No meaningful value chain restructuring is plausible within the investment horizon
- Industry cost structure is determined by physical inputs that AI cannot fundamentally alter

**🔮 Forward-looking acceleration prompts for this dimension:**
- Are upstream or downstream players investing in AI that could enable them to vertically integrate into this company's portion of the value chain?
- Could AI-driven pricing transparency compress margins industry-wide (e.g., AI comparison tools, automated procurement)?
- If the company's value-add is primarily cognitive (analysis, coordination, quality control), how quickly could AI replicate that layer?

## Dimension 3: New AI-Native Products/Services

Assesses whether AI is creating entirely new product/service categories that could substitute for or make obsolete the company's current offerings. This focuses on the product innovation threat rather than competitive entry.

### Decision Rules

**Score 5 — ALL of the following are true:**
- AI can directly replicate the company's core product/service at comparable quality (e.g., AI replacing human-written reports, AI-generated designs, AI-powered diagnostics)
- Major AI labs (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta) are actively investing in capabilities that target this domain
- Early AI alternatives are already available and improving rapidly (quality doubling within 12-18 months)
- Customer expectations are visibly shifting toward AI-native alternatives
- The company's current offering will be considered outdated within 3-5 years without reinvention

**Score 4 — AT LEAST THREE of the following are true:**
- AI can produce a materially better or cheaper version of core elements of the product/service
- AI research investment targeting this domain is growing significantly
- Some customer segments are already adopting AI alternatives for partial replacement
- Company must reinvent or significantly AI-augment its offering within hold period
- >30% of the company's product/service value chain can be AI-automated

**Score 3 — AT LEAST TWO of the following are true:**
- AI enhances the product/service but does not fundamentally change what it is
- Customers expect incremental AI features (e.g., recommendations, personalization) but core offering remains relevant
- AI can automate 10-30% of the product/service value chain
- Some product evolution needed but core offering is durable for the hold period

**Score 2 — AT LEAST ONE of the following is true:**
- AI improves HOW the product/service is delivered (efficiency, quality) but not WHAT it is
- Core offering is physically or structurally durable (requires physical materials, equipment, or in-person delivery)
- AI advancement in this domain faces fundamental barriers (physical world, regulation, trust)
- <10% of the product/service value chain can be AI-automated

**Score 1 — ALL of the following are true:**
- Product/service is fundamentally physical — requires materials, equipment, and/or in-person human execution
- No AI system can replicate the core value proposition (physical transformation, regulated procedure, trust-based relationship)
- AI capability advancement in this domain faces multi-year barriers (robotics readiness, regulatory approval, safety requirements)
- Customer expectations are unchanged by AI — they value physical delivery, human expertise, or regulatory compliance

**🔮 Forward-looking acceleration prompts for this dimension:**
- Are AI labs (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta) actively investing in capabilities that target this product/service domain?
- Could AI create a "good enough" alternative that captures the low-complexity segment of this company's market, even if it can't replace the full offering?
- Are customers in adjacent industries already adopting AI alternatives for similar services — could that behavior spread?

## Dimension 4: New AI-Native Entrants

Assesses whether AI-native companies are entering the company's existing product/service market and gaining traction. This focuses on the competitive threat from new entrants rather than product substitution.

### Decision Rules

**Score 5 — ALL of the following are true:**
- At least one AI-native competitor has achieved >10% market share AND is growing >20% YoY
- Customers are explicitly evaluating providers based on AI/technology capabilities in RFPs or purchasing decisions
- Traditional competitive advantages (brand, relationships, physical presence) are demonstrably losing relevance
- Companies without AI capabilities are losing market share at measurable rates
- Industry conferences, trade press, and analyst reports describe a fundamental competitive shift

**Score 4 — AT LEAST THREE of the following are true:**
- AI-native entrants exist with meaningful traction (>$50M revenue or >3% market share)
- Leading incumbents are making significant AI investments (>5% of revenue) and gaining competitive advantage
- New competitive dimensions (data quality, AI sophistication, speed of deployment) are becoming important
- At least one major competitor has publicly repositioned around AI
- Talent competition has shifted toward AI/ML skills in the industry

**Score 3 — AT LEAST TWO of the following are true:**
- AI-native entrants exist but have not yet achieved significant scale
- Multiple incumbents are piloting AI but no clear competitive gap has emerged yet
- AI is becoming one factor in competition but traditional advantages still dominate
- Industry is in early/mid stages of AI adoption with experimentation but no clear winners

**Score 2 — AT LEAST ONE of the following is true:**
- Industry competition is still driven by traditional factors (physical assets, regulatory licenses, relationships, brand)
- AI maturity is uniformly low across all competitors (no competitive gap)
- AI-native entrants exist but face fundamental barriers to scaling (regulatory, physical, trust)
- No measurable shift in how customers evaluate or choose providers

**Score 1 — ALL of the following are true:**
- No AI-native competitors exist in the industry
- No incumbent has deployed AI at meaningful scale
- Industry competitive dynamics are unchanged by AI
- Physical, regulatory, or relationship barriers dominate and are unaffected by AI
- Customer purchasing decisions are entirely driven by non-technology factors

**🔮 Forward-looking acceleration prompts for this dimension:**
- Are AI-native startups raising funding in adjacent sectors that could easily pivot to this company's market?
- Are incumbents in this industry beginning to differentiate on AI/technology capabilities — even if no clear competitive gap has emerged yet?
- Could a well-funded AI-native entrant bypass traditional barriers (physical presence, relationships) by combining AI with a marketplace or platform model?

## Aggregate Disruption Risk Scoring

**Total Disruption Score** = Sum of 4 dimension scores (each 1-5). Range: 4-20.

**Decision Rule (Disruption Impact):**
- **Low (4-9)**: Limited disruption pressure; mostly incremental change
- **Medium (10-14)**: Material disruption pressure; adaptation required and should be underwritten
- **High (15-20)**: Severe disruption pressure; reinvention or repricing required

### Dimension Differentiation Requirement

After scoring all four dimensions, **verify that you have not scored all four dimensions within ±1 of each other** (e.g., all 2s, or a mix of 2s and 3s). The AI disruption threat landscape is rarely uniform across all four dimensions — most companies are more exposed in some areas than others.

**If all four dimensions score within ±1 of each other**, you must re-examine at least one dimension and articulate specifically why the company is equally insulated (or equally exposed) across all four. For most companies, at least one dimension should diverge meaningfully. Common patterns:

- Physical service companies often score 1-2 on Business Model and New AI-Native Products but higher on Value Chain Disruption (AI can restructure how work is dispatched, priced, or sourced)
- Information-heavy companies often score higher on Business Model Disruption and New AI-Native Products but lower on Value Chain (physical assets protect the chain)
- B2B companies with deep customer relationships may score low on New AI-Native Entrants but higher on New AI-Native Products/Services (AI alternatives to their offerings)

This requirement ensures the scorer thinks critically about WHERE disruption risk lives rather than applying a flat default across all dimensions.

## Decisioning Matrix (v4)

The Value Creation Potential (AI Score) and Disruption Impact scores are combined in a matrix:

**VC Score Thresholds (out of 55 max, Tier 3 weighted 3×):**
- **VC Low**: 11-25
- **VC Medium**: 26-40
- **VC High**: 41-55

**DR Score Thresholds (out of 20 max):**
- **Low Disruption**: 4-9
- **Medium Disruption**: 10-14
- **High Disruption**: 15-20

| VC Potential | High Disruption | Medium Disruption | Low Disruption |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High** | Avoid | Pursue | Pursue |
| **Medium** | Avoid | Monitor | Pursue |
| **Low** | Avoid | Avoid | Avoid |

**Interpretation:**
- **Pursue**: Meaningful AI value creation opportunity with manageable disruption risk — invest and build AI capabilities
- **Monitor**: Moderate AI value creation with moderate disruption — worth tracking and selectively investing as AI capabilities evolve
- **Avoid**: Either high disruption risk that overwhelms value creation potential, OR low AI opportunity regardless of disruption level — AI is not a viable thesis

**Key Philosophy Change (v3 → v4):**
In v3, high disruption + high VC was "Top Priority" — the logic being that AI was both a massive opportunity AND a defensive necessity. In v4, the framework takes a more conservative view: high disruption risk is treated as a fundamental threat that cannot be reliably mitigated through value creation alone. Companies facing severe disruption pressure are classified as "Avoid" regardless of VC potential, because the disruption risk may destroy the investment before value creation can be realized.

## Disruption Scoring Process Checklist

For each dimension, the scorer must:
1. Review the quantitative decision rules and check which threshold is met
2. Assign a score (1-5) with specific evidence cited for each criterion checked
3. Write a 2-3 sentence rationale citing specific data points
4. Identify the most important single piece of evidence supporting the score
5. Note any assumptions that could change the score by ±1 point
